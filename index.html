CASESTUDY:MISLEADING VISUALIZATION ON GLOBAL WARMING


By Danielle Wang

On Dec. 15 2015, National Review published a tweet with a line chart named "average annual global temperature in Fahrenheit", which was criticized as "the bad chart heard around the (graphics) world".

In this line chart, National Review want to show the change of average annual global temperature from 1880 to 2015. The y-axis represents temperature starting from zero. In general, temperature in these 135 years keeps rising and the change was within five degrees. A change of even one degree in the average global temperature is significant, but starting this chart at zero makes it look miniscule. What we can see from this chart is a smooth line, which gave the misleading information of slow growth.

If we want to accurately represent the change in the global average temperature since 1880, we should start the y-axis at a higher number. In this case, the significant rise of five degrees can be showed clearly.

According to Quartz , many people complain about their charts because` the y-axis doesn’t start at zero. From their point of views, truncating the y-axis, as we often do in line charts, exaggerates what the data really represent. Some people consider it a maxim that the y-axis should always be zeroed. Otherwise, they think it is lying with data visualization.

Nevertheless, It’s OK not to start the y-axis at zero for better storytelling. Charts should convey information and make a point. We make charts to illustrate ideas that have context beyond their x- and y-axes. Forcing the y-axis to start at zero can do just as much to obscure and confuse the point as the opposite.

“Shut up about the y-axis. It shouldn’t always start at zero,” reads a vox.com headline. Sometimes, you have to start the y-axis somewhere other than zero to show appropriate context such as the global warming case.
